What we actually check

WHAT COUNTS AS COMPLIANT, AND WHAT DOESN'T.

✓

Clear compensation language

Has to state the paid relationship plainly, like "#ad," "sponsored by," or "I earn a commission." Not enough: vague support language like "thanks to" or "collab," which the FTC treats as non-compliant on its own.

✓

Placed before the fold

Instagram and TikTok truncate captions around 125 characters before the "more" tap. A disclosure buried past that point doesn't count as conspicuous, even if it's there.

✓

Spoken or on-screen in video, near the start

On YouTube and TikTok, the disclosure needs to land close to when the sponsored content actually appears, not buried in an outro. A "sponsor" mention at minute 38 of a 39-minute YouTube video is a real case we caught that didn't hold up.

✓

Platform tags help, but don't replace disclosure

Instagram's "Paid Partnership" label, TikTok's branded-content toggle, and YouTube's "Includes paid promotion" checkbox are good signals, but not enough on their own if the caption or spoken content never says it too.

✓

YouTube: we check what's said, not just what's written

YouTube has no ground-truth partnership flag available to a third-party checker, so most tools only scan the video description. We also scan the transcript, since creators often disclose out loud even when the description misses it entirely.

✓

Survives the repost

A caption compliant on Instagram can lose its disclosure entirely when re-cut for TikTok. That's a new violation, not a carried-over pass. We check both sides, not just one.
